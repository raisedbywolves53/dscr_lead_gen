# Real Estate Agent as DSCR Referral Partner: ICP Deep Dive, Discovery Methods & Partnership Models

## Executive Summary

Investor-focused real estate agents represent one of the highest-leverage referral channels for loan officers operating in the DSCR space. Investors now account for between 13% and 33% of residential home purchases depending on the dataset and timeframe, and real estate agents influence approximately 45% of buyer lender decisions. The opportunity to build an LO–agent–investor triangle — where the LO provides scored leads and the agent provides warm introductions — is well-supported by market dynamics. This report covers how investor-focused agents operate, how to identify and locate them, the REIA ecosystem, RESPA compliance constraints, partnership mechanics, approach tactics, and a market-by-market breakdown across 12 target geographies.[^1][^2][^3]

***

## Part 1: How Investor-Focused Agents Operate

### Market Share of Investor Transactions

Real estate investors accounted for **13% of all home purchases** in 2024, up slightly from 12.7% in 2023. Some data sources (using different methodologies and including LLC-entity buyers) peg investor activity significantly higher — at 20-25% of purchases nationally, and as high as 33% of single-family home sales in Q2 2025. Missouri, Oklahoma, and Kansas consistently rank as the highest investor-activity states, with Missouri reaching 21.2% investor buyer share in 2024. Georgia had the highest investor seller share at 15.9%. Small investors now dominate the landscape: those owning 10 or fewer properties represent 59% of all investor purchases, a record high, while large institutional investors (1,000+ properties) make up just 2% of investor-owned homes.[^4][^5][^6][^3][^1]

Critically, for the purposes of targeting DSCR financing, the most relevant segment is the **debt-using small investor**. All-cash investor sales fell to the lowest level since 2008 in 2024, meaning more investors than ever are seeking financing. This creates a structural tailwind for DSCR product adoption.[^6][^1]

### How Agents Serve Investors vs. Owner-Occupants

Investor-focused agents operate differently from residential agents serving owner-occupants. They typically source clients through REIA meetups, BiggerPockets profiles and forums, property management referrals, and direct social media outreach positioning themselves as "investor-friendly". These agents must understand investment-specific concepts — cap rates, DSCR thresholds, NOI calculations, LLC structuring — that are largely irrelevant to residential buyers.[^7][^8][^9]

The typical NAR-member agent closes about **10 transactions per year** (median), with a median sales volume of $2.5 million. However, agent productivity is extremely bimodal: the top 20% of agents average 26 transactions per year, while the bottom 80% average just 3.5. Investor-focused agents who work with repeat buyers — especially those acquiring portfolios of 5-15 properties — can substantially exceed the 10-deal median, as each satisfied investor client often transacts multiple times per year.[^10][^11]

### Lender Influence and Financing Decisions

A critical structural insight: **real estate agents influence approximately 45% of buyer lender decisions**. The McKinsey 2018 Retail Banking study found 39% of first-time buyers and 27% of repeat buyers researched and chose a lender through their agent. This figure is likely even higher for investors, who lean on their agent network more heavily than owner-occupant buyers due to the complexity of investment financing and the speed required to win deals.[^2][^12]

Investor agents typically develop a preferred lender relationship or small roster of go-to lenders. These are not purely transactional referrals — the agent's reputation is attached to the quality of the closing. As a result, agents prefer lenders who are **fast, communicative, and reliable** above all else.[^13][^2]

### Commission Structure and Repeat Business Value

Real estate referral fees between agents are typically 25-40% of gross commission income (GCI), with 25% being the most common rate. For investor agents, the real upside isn't the per-deal fee but the **lifetime value of a repeat investor client**: an investor acquiring 3-5 properties per year generates ongoing commission, referrals within the investor community, and potential property management referral fees.[^14][^15]

Property management referrals are a secondary income stream: agents who help a buyer acquire a rental property can refer them to a property management company and receive a flat fee or first-month's rent percentage. This creates a long feedback loop where the agent earns on acquisition, management placement, and eventual resale. However, referral arrangements must be carefully structured; conflicts of interest can arise when PM referral fees are not disclosed.[^16][^14]

## Part 2: Identifying Investment-Focused Agents

### NAR Designations as Investor Signals

Not all designations signal investor activity equally. The table below maps each to its actual relevance for identifying investment-focused agents:

| Designation | Full Name | Investor Signal Strength | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|--------------------------|-------|
| **CCIM** | Certified Commercial Investment Member | ★★★★★ | 200 classroom hours, 13,000-member global network; explicitly covers investment and commercial RE[^17][^18] |
| **CRE** | Counselor of Real Estate | ★★★★★ | Invitation-only elite group; institutional-grade investment focus[^19] |
| **CIPS** | Certified International Property Specialist | ★★★ | International focus with some investor overlap; strong in markets with foreign investor activity[^19] |
| **ABR** | Accredited Buyer's Representative | ★ | General buyer representation; no investment focus[^18] |
| **RSPS** | Resort & Second-Home Property Specialist | ★★ | Short-term rental/vacation property focus; moderate overlap with investors in STR markets[^19] |
| **SFR** | Short Sales & Foreclosure Resource | ★★ | Distressed property expertise; overlaps with value-add investors but primarily serves distressed sellers, not portfolio builders[^20][^21] |
| **SRES** | Senior Real Estate Specialist | ★ | Downsizing/senior focus; no investor signal[^19] |

**Key insight**: CCIM is the strongest single credential indicator for investment-grade agent activity. An agent with CCIM is not just "designations collecting" — it requires 200 hours of coursework, portfolio documentation, and peer review. Agents who self-describe as "investor-friendly" on their Google My Business profile, BiggerPockets agent listing, or LinkedIn headline are more actionable signals for the DSCR segment than most formal designations.[^17]

### MLS and Transaction Pattern Signals

MLS data provides the richest signal set for identifying investor-active agents. Key filters to apply when mining MLS data or using platforms like BatchLeads, Propstream, or ATTOM:

- **Cash transaction frequency**: Agents who repeatedly appear on cash purchase closings (especially in investor-dense zip codes) are almost certainly working with investors. MLS records typically flag "cash" as the financing type in closing data.[^22][^23]
- **Non-owner-occupied patterns**: When a buyer's mailing address does not match the purchased property address, it indicates an investor (non-owner-occupant). Multiple such transactions by the same buyer agent is a strong signal.[^23]
- **LLC buyer representation**: Agents representing LLCs in purchase transactions are by definition working with investment buyers. LLC transaction records are public in most states.
- **High volume in specific zip codes**: Investor-heavy zip codes (often characterized by high cap rates, lower price points, high cash transaction rates) attract a disproportionate share of investor agents.

### Online and Social Signals

The most efficient discovery methods for investor agents outside MLS data:

- **BiggerPockets Agent Finder**: A purpose-built tool matching investors with investor-friendly agents. It serves 135,000+ investors and is backed by a Mashvisor data partnership. Agents who actively maintain a BiggerPockets profile and Agent Finder listing have self-selected as investor-focused.[^8][^24]
- **REIA Membership Directories**: REIA chapters often maintain member and vendor directories. Agents who pay to attend or sponsor REIA events are signaling market orientation.
- **"Investor-Friendly Agent" Google search**: A simple search for "[city] investor-friendly real estate agent" or "[city] DSCR agent" surfaces agents who have explicitly optimized their web presence for this audience.
- **Facebook Groups**: Active participation in investor-focused Facebook groups (e.g., market-specific wholesaling and flipping groups, BiggerPockets Facebook community) is a strong signal. These are often city-specific and publicly searchable.
- **LinkedIn**: Searching LinkedIn for "investment properties," "investor-friendly," "DSCR," or "rental portfolio" in agent profiles within a metro is a scalable discovery method.
- **YouTube/TikTok**: Agents who produce investor-education content (deal analysis, market tours of investment properties, "how to analyze a rental") have built explicit investment audiences.

### Brokerage Signals

Certain brokerage environments are more likely to concentrate investor-focused agents:

- **Keller Williams**: Strong internal training culture. KW agents who complete the investment-focused training modules and team systems are more likely to serve investors. KW's profit-sharing model rewards agents who recruit, which creates tight-knit investment teams.[^25]
- **eXp Realty**: The largest brokerage in the US with ~82,000 agents as of early 2025. eXp's cloud-based model attracts entrepreneurial agents, and several investor-focused teams operate within eXp. Revenue share model incentivizes agents to attract other productive agents.[^26]
- **RE/MAX Commercial**: RE/MAX's commercial division explicitly serves investment and commercial buyers.
- **Boutique investment-focused brokerages**: In every major metro, there are typically 2-4 boutique firms that specialize exclusively in investor transactions. These are often the highest-value partnership targets.
- **Independent brokerages with PM arms**: Firms that combine brokerage and property management services are almost always investor-centric.

## Part 3: The REIA Ecosystem

### National Overview

The **National REIA** (National Real Estate Investors Association) operates as the umbrella non-profit, with **over 120 local chapters and 40,000+ members** across the United States, founded in 1985. This represents a significant but not exhaustive share of REIA activity — thousands of independent, non-affiliated investor groups operate separately. The Texas REIAs network alone claims 140,000 members across its state-wide network.[^27][^28]

REIA meetings typically occur monthly at hotel conference rooms, with 50–300 attendees depending on market size and chapter maturity. Attendee mix includes: beginner and intermediate investors (largest share), experienced portfolio investors, wholesalers, fix-and-flip operators, real estate agents, property managers, hard money lenders, title companies, insurance agents, and contractors. Vendors and service providers pay to attend or sponsor in order to access this concentrated buyer pool.

### Sponsorship Tiers and Vendor Costs

REIA sponsorship is one of the most cost-effective ways to reach investor-focused agents AND the investors themselves. Typical tiers based on available data:

| Tier | Annual Cost (Typical) | Includes |
|------|----------------------|----------|
| Silver | $250–$1,000/yr | Logo on website/banner, social media mention, newsletter blast |
| Bronze | $1,000/yr | Above + sponsor booth at 1 live event, 2–5 team memberships |
| Gold | $2,500/yr | Above + 2–5 minute speech at a live event, 10 team memberships, first right of refusal on large events[^29] |

Beyond annual sponsorships, trade show tables at annual REIA conferences (like JaxREIA's annual summit) typically run **$650–$800 per table** for a 6-foot table and two admissions. Regular monthly meeting attendance for a non-member typically runs $20–$40/meeting. Vendor memberships, which allow up to 4 company representatives to attend at member pricing, typically cost $200/year.[^30][^31][^32]

The Gold-tier REIA sponsorship — particularly the speaking slot — is the most valuable single touchpoint. A 2–5 minute presentation to 100-300 investors and agents in a single evening provides brand legitimacy, lead generation, and immediate networking opportunity that would cost exponentially more through paid digital channels.

## Part 4: The LO–Agent–Investor Triangle

### Current State of Deal Flow Sharing

In the existing investor ecosystem, loan officers and real estate agents share deal flow primarily through **co-marketing relationships and warm referrals**. **96% of top loan officers identify real estate agent referrals as their strongest business source**. The flow typically runs one direction: agent sends clients to LO for pre-qualification and financing. The more sophisticated arrangement — where the LO also generates investor leads and feeds them back to an agent partner — is less common but represents a significant competitive differentiation opportunity.[^64]

Referral fees between LOs and agents are prohibited under RESPA Section 8 — it is illegal to give or receive anything of value in exchange for referring settlement service business involving a federally related mortgage loan. However, **RESPA explicitly allows co-marketing arrangements** (joint ads, shared educational events, co-branded materials) as long as each party pays their proportionate share of costs and receives genuine value from the arrangement.[^65][^66][^67][^68]

There is one notable existing model of direct LO-to-agent referral fee payment: **Easy Street Capital pays real estate agents 0.50% of loan amount on closed DSCR loans** — approximately $2,500 per loan on their average $500K loan size. This program works because Easy Street operates as a non-bank lender outside certain RESPA structures, but any LO operating at a federally-regulated institution should consult legal counsel before replicating this model.[^69]

### What Makes an LO-Agent Partnership Sticky

Research and practitioner experience consistently point to the same drivers of durable LO-agent relationships:[^70][^64][^13]

1. **Communication frequency and quality**: Proactive milestone updates (not just when problems arise) are the single most commonly cited factor. Agents recommend lenders who keep them and their clients informed throughout the process.
2. **Reliability and pre-approval speed**: Agents need to know their clients can get funded. Fast pre-approvals (24-48 hours) allow agents to make competitive offers with financing confidence.
3. **Product depth**: An LO who can offer DSCR *and* conventional *and* bridge loans in one relationship is more valuable than a single-product specialist.
4. **Value-add beyond transactions**: LOs who co-host investor education events, provide market reports, or share deal analysis tools become embedded in the agent's practice rather than being transactional vendors.
5. **Reciprocity**: The partnership becomes sticky when the LO **also generates value for the agent** — not just receives referrals. This is where the scored investor lead program creates a structural advantage.

### How the Lead Platform Fits the Triangle

The B2B DSCR lead platform creates a unique and compelling insertion point. Here is how the operational model works:

1. **LO subscribes to the scored lead platform** and receives a pipeline of enriched investor leads (property owners with indicators of DSCR financing need — multi-unit ownership, high equity, LLC title, portfolio acquisition patterns).
2. **LO reaches out to investor leads** with relevant, data-backed outreach: "I noticed you own [X properties], have you considered a DSCR refinance to pull equity from [Address]?"
3. **LO shares select warm leads with agent partner**: Investors who indicate they are looking to acquire additional properties are passed to the agent, along with relevant data context. The LO says: "I have an investor client looking in your market — want to work with them together?"
4. **Agent connects investor to deal flow** and the LO handles the financing, creating a closed-loop triangle where all three parties win.

This model functions as a **co-marketing arrangement** under RESPA — the LO is not paying the agent for referrals but instead sharing mutually valuable business intelligence that creates co-marketing opportunities. The key compliance principle: no direct payment flows from LO to agent for referrals, but both parties invest in a shared pipeline and marketing relationship.[^66][^71]

### Existing Lead-Sharing Partnership Models

Several documented models already exist in the market:

- **Homebot Partner Intel**: A co-marketing platform that gives LOs AI-powered visibility into agent client engagement and automates branded touchpoints. LOs using Homebot to co-sponsor agents get their name in front of 330 additional clients on average, leading to 50% more client conversations.[^64]
- **Co-branded investor education workshops**: LOs and investor agents co-host educational events (REIA presentations, online webinars) where each brings their own audience. No money exchanges hands, both parties market to the combined audience.
- **Joint MLS data review sessions**: LO and agent meet regularly to review investor market activity, identify active investor buyers, and proactively reach out with relevant financing intelligence.

## Part 5: Approaching and Converting Agent Partners

### Outreach Channels by Effectiveness

| Channel | Reach | Quality | Cost | Best For |
|---------|-------|---------|------|----------|
| REIA Events (in-person) | Medium | Very High | Low–Medium | Brand building, warm introductions |
| LinkedIn (direct outreach) | High | Medium | Low | Scalable initial contact |
| Brokerage Lunch-and-Learns | Low–Medium | High | Low | Deep relationship with agent teams |
| BiggerPockets (profile + forums) | Medium | High | Low | Credibility, investor-adjacent agents |
| Email sequences | High | Low–Medium | Low | Volume prospecting |
| Facebook Groups | Medium | Medium | Low | Community credibility |
| Cold calls to agents | High | Low | Low | Pipeline volume, low conversion |

The highest-ROI channel for the DSCR lead platform context is the **brokerage lunch-and-learn** combined with **REIA speaking slots**. Lunch-and-learns allow deep, uninterrupted education time with a captured audience. REIA speaking slots build market-wide credibility with the most investor-active professionals in a given city.

### What Agents Care About (Messaging Framework)

Investor-focused agents' core motivations, in priority order based on market research:

1. **More qualified investor clients**: An agent's biggest constraint is finding investors who are deal-ready, pre-qualified, and serious. Any tool or relationship that improves client quality wins immediately.
2. **Faster and more reliable closings**: Investor deals are time-sensitive — delayed closings damage agent reputation and relationships. Speed and certainty are paramount.[^2][^13]
3. **Competitive differentiation**: Agents who can offer clients access to scored investor leads or financing intelligence position themselves above generalist competitors.
4. **Repeat and referral business**: Investors transact frequently and refer other investors. One well-served investor client can generate 5–10 future transactions.
5. **Education and authority**: Agents who co-host investment education content position themselves as the local expert, attracting inbound investor clients.

The most resonant pitch for an investor-focused agent is not "I offer DSCR loans" — it's: *"I can give you a reason to reach out to your investor clients today, with data on their portfolio that most agents don't have access to."*

### Agent Objections to New Lender Partnerships

Common objections and how to address them:

**"I already have a go-to lender."**
→ This is the most common objection. The response is not to ask them to replace their existing LO, but to position the platform as additive: *"I'm not asking you to replace anyone. I'm asking to be your specialist lender for the deals your current LO can't do — investors who need DSCR because their income doesn't qualify for conventional."*

**"I've worked with LOs before and they go quiet after a few referrals."**
→ This reflects past relationship failure. The response is process: *"Here's exactly what you can expect from me on every deal — a 24-hour pre-approval, status updates at every milestone, and a direct line when anything comes up."*

**"DSCR is too complicated for my clients."**
→ This reflects an education gap. The response is simplification: *"DSCR doesn't require tax returns or W-2s. If the property cash flows, we can usually get it done. Let me do a 10-minute walkthrough — you'll see it's simpler than conventional for your investor clients."*

**"I don't have time for a new partnership."**
→ The response is low commitment entry: *"I'm not asking for exclusivity. Just give me one deal to show you what I can do. If I close it clean and fast, we can talk about whether there's more to build here."*

### Partnership Development Timeline

Based on industry experience and available data, the realistic timeline from first contact to first closed referral looks like this:[^72][^73]

| Phase | Timeframe | Activity |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Initial outreach and introduction | Weeks 1–2 | LinkedIn, REIA event, cold email |
| First meeting / coffee | Weeks 2–4 | Relationship building, product education |
| Education and trust building | Months 1–2 | Co-hosted event, deal analysis sessions |
| First referral or warm introduction | Months 2–3 | Agent refers an investor client or accepts a lead |
| First closed deal | Months 3–5 | Closing reinforces relationship, starts pipeline |
| Durable active partnership | Month 6+ | Regular lead sharing, co-marketing, monthly check-ins |

Active REIA networking and brokerage lunch-and-learns can compress the front end of this timeline significantly. A well-positioned presentation at a REIA meeting where the LO clearly demonstrates value has generated first introductions within days.

### Retention Strategies for Active Partners

- **Regular lead sharing**: Consistent delivery of investor leads (even 2-3 per month) keeps the agent engaged and reminds them of the partnership value.
- **Deal celebration**: Personally recognizing every closed deal — even a handwritten card or a social media tag — reinforces the relationship and generates public referrals.
- **Co-marketing content**: Monthly co-branded market updates sent to both audiences provide ongoing value without requiring transactional activity.
- **Exclusive market access**: Offering specific agents a "first look" at leads in their geographic focus area creates perceived scarcity and loyalty.
- **Quarterly business review**: A 30-minute check-in reviewing leads shared, deals closed, and pipeline outlook shows professionalism and builds long-term commitment.

## Part 6: Agent ICP by Market

There are approximately **1.45–1.52 million NAR Realtors** in the United States as of 2024–2025, with an estimated 3 million total licensed agents including non-NAR members. Across these 12 markets, the investment-focus share of agents varies significantly.[^74][^75][^76][^77][^78]

### Raleigh-Durham, NC

The Triangle market has experienced 10.4% population growth since 2019 and continues attracting significant in-migration from Washington DC, New York, and San Francisco. The median home price in Raleigh sits at approximately **$451,000** with 6.1% YOY growth, while Wake County's November 2025 median was $470,000. Triangle home sales rose 13.2% in 2025.[^79][^80][^81][^82]

**Agent landscape**: The Triangle REALTOR ecosystem is large and growing, with active investor-agent communities concentrated around the NCREIA ecosystem. **Key REIA chapters**: NCREIA Raleigh (monthly, major hub), Triangle REIA (TREIA) in Cary. **Dominant brokerages**: Keller Williams (multiple Triangle offices), Coldwell Banker Howard Perry & Walston, Howard Hanna, and local boutique investment-focused firms. **Investor dynamics**: The Research Triangle's strong tech and biotech economy drives consistent demand for both owner-occupant and investor properties. The market sees significant LLC purchasing activity in suburban neighborhoods around Cary, Apex, Morrisville, and Northeast Raleigh. **Agent outreach**: NCREIA meetings (Raleigh chapter), Triangle real estate investor Facebook groups, BiggerPockets profiles, and KW investment team lunch-and-learns.[^34][^83][^38]

### Charlotte, NC

Charlotte has solidified itself as a top-three U.S. city for investor returns combining appreciation and cash flow. CRE investment volume exceeded $8 billion in 2025, up 24% year-over-year. The median home price is approximately **$437,500**, up 3.7% YOY, with 117 people moving to Charlotte daily.[^84][^85][^86]

**Agent landscape**: Charlotte has a dense investor-agent community, particularly in the banking corridor and southern suburbs (Ballantyne, Fort Mill). **Key REIA chapters**: NCREIA Charlotte (monthly), Charlotte REIA (1st Thursday + weekly breakfast meetings), Metrolina REIA (3rd Thursday). **Dominant brokerages**: RE/MAX Executive, eXp Realty Charlotte, Allen Tate, and boutique investment firms. **Investor dynamics**: Charlotte's banking sector (Bank of America, Wells Fargo) creates a stable pool of high-income professionals looking to invest; out-of-state investors from New York, DC, and California are active buyers. **Agent outreach**: Charlotte REIA sponsorship (speaking slot) is high-value; the Charlotte investor Facebook communities are active.[^37][^36][^38]

### Cleveland, OH

Cleveland is among the **highest-yielding real estate markets in the United States** with a gross rental yield of 11.3% and a median home price of approximately **$175,000**. It is consistently ranked as a top cash-flow market. The healthcare sector (Cleveland Clinic, University Hospitals, MetroHealth) provides stable employment-driven rental demand.[^87][^88][^89]

**Agent landscape**: Cleveland has a concentrated investor-agent community, particularly in Greater Cleveland and eastern suburbs. **Key REIA chapters**: Great Lakes REIA (Cleveland, 3rd Thursday at Crowne Plaza Cleveland South), Greater Cleveland Real Estate Investor Association (active Meetup, weekly virtual networking), OREIA statewide network with 15 chapters. **Dominant brokerages**: RE/MAX Haven Realty, eXp Realty Ohio, Howard Hanna. **Investor dynamics**: Cleveland investors are predominantly cash-flow focused with very low acquisition prices; DSCR financing is highly relevant given the strength of rent-to-value ratios. Out-of-state investors are increasingly active. **Agent outreach**: Great Lakes REIA speaking sponsorship; OREIA annual summit.[^45][^46][^47]

### Philadelphia, PA

Philadelphia offers median listing prices of approximately **$290,000** with metro-area sale prices around $416,000. The city ranks 5th most competitive metro for buyers in 2025. Strong DSCR-friendly fundamentals: properties in stable neighborhoods generate $1,500–$2,200/month in rent on purchases under $200,000. The life sciences, healthcare, and tech corridor (Comcast, Jefferson Health, UPenn) sustains rental demand year-round.[^90][^91]

**Agent landscape**: Philadelphia has a large investor-agent community spanning the city itself and surrounding suburbs. **Key REIA chapters**: DIG (Diversified Real Estate Investor Group, Glenside/Montgomery County), Delco Property Investors, Montgomery County Investors Group. **Dominant brokerages**: Elfant Wissahickon, KW Philly, Compass, and several boutique investment-focused firms in neighborhoods like Fishtown and Northern Liberties. **Investor dynamics**: Bridge-to-DSCR strategies are common in Philadelphia's value-add multifamily market, particularly for investors repositioning 2-4 unit properties. **Agent outreach**: Philadelphia-area REIA events, LinkedIn outreach to agents in high-investor zip codes (Fishtown, Kensington, West Philadelphia).[^92][^49]

### Pittsburgh, PA

Pittsburgh's median home price is approximately **$250,000** (May 2025), with properties available under $250,000 in many desirable neighborhoods. Key economic drivers: UPMC, Google, Carnegie Mellon University, and the tech sector create strong rental demand. The market is transitioning to a buyer-friendly environment with longer days-on-market (62 days as of April 2025), creating negotiating room for investor buyers.[^93][^94]

**Agent landscape**: Pittsburgh has two primary REIA organizations. **Key REIA chapters**: ACRE of Pittsburgh (1st Tuesday, DoubleTree Greentree), Western PA REIA / PREIA (3rd Tuesday, Greater Pittsburgh Masonic Center, Josh Caldwell). Pittsburgh REIAs are known for experienced membership and active deal flow. **Dominant brokerages**: Howard Hanna (largest in Pittsburgh), RE/MAX Realty, Berkshire Hathaway. **Agent outreach**: ACRE and PREIA are the two primary entry points; both are active and welcoming to LO vendors.[^50][^49]

### Indianapolis, IN

Indianapolis is an NAR top-10 hottest housing market for 2025, with 6.8% average rental yield and 42% of homes priced below $236,000. The market benefits from strong job growth and a reduced mortgage lock-in effect relative to other metros.[^88][^95]

**Agent landscape**: Indianapolis has a well-organized investor community. **Key REIA chapters**: CIREIA (Central Indiana REIA) — the largest REIA in Indiana, meeting the 1st Thursday 5:30–9pm at CIREIA Real Estate Campus. Indiana State REIA provides statewide legislative advocacy. **Dominant brokerages**: F.C. Tucker, RE/MAX, KW Indianapolis. **Investor dynamics**: Indianapolis has attracted significant out-of-state investor activity, particularly from East Coast and West Coast investors seeking cash-flow properties. DSCR adoption is strong given the favorable rent-to-value ratios. **Agent outreach**: CIREIA sponsorship is the most direct access point; the Indiana Association of Realtors (18,000+ members) hosts events with investor-focused segments.[^59][^58]

## Compliance Summary: RESPA and Co-Marketing

RESPA Section 8(a) strictly prohibits giving or receiving **anything of value** in exchange for the referral of settlement service business involving a federally related mortgage loan. This prohibition covers:[^67][^68][^65]

- Direct referral fees from an LO to a real estate agent
- Paying an agent per lead referred
- Providing discounts, gifts, or benefits conditioned on referrals

**What IS permitted under RESPA**:
- **Co-marketing arrangements** where each party pays their proportionate share of costs (joint advertising, co-branded brochures, shared email campaigns) and each receives genuine value[^65][^66]
- **Educational events** co-hosted by LO and agent, shared equally in cost and benefit
- **Lead sharing** without payment — the LO providing investor lead data to an agent partner without any fee exchange is not a RESPA violation if no quid pro quo exists
- **Affiliated Business Arrangements (AfBAs)** with proper disclosure under Section 8(c)(4), but these require disclosure forms at time of referral

The B2B scored lead platform model — where the LO subscribes to a data service, receives leads, and shares warm introductions with agent partners without payment flow — is designed to operate within RESPA compliance. The LO-agent relationship is mutually beneficial but not a payment-for-referral structure. Any LO should consult with their compliance officer or legal counsel before formalizing co-marketing agreements.
